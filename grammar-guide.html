Rule 1

The It / They Rule

Two Types of Verb Questions on the SAT

Type A

Subject–Verb Agreement

The verb form changes to match the subject — singular or plural. No need to read these questions at all!

✓ Use the It / They Rule
Type B

Verb Tense

The verb changes to reflect when something happened — past, present, future.

✎ You must read the sentence

How to Spot a Verb Tense Question

Look at the answer choices. If any of these four appear, it's a tense question — read it carefully.

-ed
past tense
-ing
alone (no helper)
will
future
to
infinitive
None of the four appear? It's a subject–verb agreement question → use the It / They Rule 🎉

The It/They rule is simple. You just choose it or they, plug that word in before every single answer choice, and choose the odd one out. Only one answer choice will be singular or plural if it's a subject verb agreement question.

Quick Decision Flow

Do any answer choices contain  -ed · -ing (alone) · will · to?
Verb Tense

Rule 2

Transitions & Semicolons: ITE

1
Spot the question type
Look at the answer choices

If every answer choice is a transition word or phrase — However, Therefore, Furthermore, Meanwhile… — it's a transition question.

✦ All choices are transitions → you're in the right place
2
Eliminate wrong answers
Every transition must be "hugged" by punctuation

It doesn't matter whether it's a comma, semicolon, period, or dash — but a transition always needs to be hugged by some kind of punctuation on at least one side. If an answer choice leaves a transition floating without punctuation, eliminate it.

✓  However, the results were surprising.
✓  The results were surprising; however, the team continued.
✗  The results were surprising however the team continued.
3
Check the sentence order
I  ·  T  ·  E

Most transition questions follow a three-sentence pattern. Use the order to figure out what kind of transition belongs in the blank.

I
Info
The first sentence introduces a fact or situation.
→
T
Transition
The second sentence is where the blank lives — it pivots or connects.
→
E
Explanation
The third sentence elaborates on or explains what was just said.
Note: There are exceptions to ITE, but it is the default pattern to look for first.
Example

Jetties—long, narrow structures that extend from a landmass into the water—are often constructed to protect coastlines from erosion. Jetties can sometimes have the opposite ______ obstructing the natural flow of sand along the shore can lead to increased erosion in some areas.

I Info T Transition E Explanation
Which choice completes the text so that it conforms to the conventions of Standard English?
A effect, though, ✓ has a ;
B effect, though; ✓ correct
C effect, though ✗ "though" not hugged after
D effect; though ✗ "though" not hugged after
Why B is correct

The passage follows the ITE pattern perfectly. The first sentence (I) gives the general fact about jetties protecting coastlines. The second sentence (T) introduces a contrast — "the opposite effect" — with though as the transition, properly hugged by a comma before and a semicolon after. The semicolon then introduces the third sentence (E), which explains how the opposite effect plays out.

4
Rare exception
When ITE doesn't apply

Very rarely a passage has only two sentences, although the first will always be Information. In this case, you won't be able to use ITE order to guide your choice.

Exception Example

Digital artist Jung (Lulu) Chen primarily uses a suite of software tools to create illustrations for children's books. To manifest the warm and welcoming atmospheres that are a signature of her ______ she occasionally relies on more traditional art techniques, such as painting with watercolors.

Which choice completes the text so that it conforms to the conventions of Standard English?
A work, though, ✓ correct
B work, though ✗ "though" not hugged after
C work; though, ✓ has a ;
D work, though; ✓ has a ;
Why this is an exception — and why A is correct

There are only two sentences here, so ITE doesn't apply. After eliminating B for leaving though un-hugged, C and D both form sentence fragments because there's no subject. You're setting up a dangling participle (more on that later) with A. Remember, information in between the commas you can always take out.

Rule 3

SuperCommas

1
The concept
What is a supercomma?

A supercomma is a semicolon used to separate items in a list when those items already contain commas. Without supercommas, the sentence becomes impossible to read.

✗  Confusing:  Paris, France, London, England, Madrid, Spain
✓  Clear:  Paris, France; London, England; Madrid, Spain
2
Spot the question type
Look for "; and" in the passage

You know it's a supercomma question when you spot a semicolon followed by "and" somewhere in the passage. This signals a three-item list is being separated by supercommas — and one supercomma is missing from the blank.

Nigerian author Buchi Emecheta's celebrated literary oeuvre includes The Joys of Motherhood, a novel about the changing roles of women in 1950s ______ a television play about the private struggles of a newlywed couple in Nigeria; and Head Above Water, her autobiography.
A) Lagos, A Kind of Marriage,
B) Lagos; A Kind of Marriage,
C) Lagos, A Kind of Marriage:
D) Lagos; A Kind of Marriage
3
Eliminate wrong answers
Cross out every choice without a semicolon

If a choice has no semicolon at all, it's automatically wrong. The semicolons are doing essential work — a choice without them can't separate the list items clearly.

A Lagos, A Kind of Marriage, ✗ no semicolon
B Lagos; A Kind of Marriage, ✓ has a ;
C Lagos, A Kind of Marriage: ✗ no semicolon
D Lagos; A Kind of Marriage ✓ has a ;
4
Choose the correct answer
Check for parallelism

Each item in the list follows the same parallel structure: a title in italics, followed by a comma, followed by its description. The semicolons fall between the three items — not inside any one of them. Try reading the list out loud with a finger up for each item; you should feel three distinct beats.

Nigerian author Buchi Emecheta's celebrated literary oeuvre includes The Joys of Motherhood, a novel about the changing roles of women in 1950s Lagos; A Kind of Marriage, a television play about the private struggles of a newlywed couple in Nigeria; and Head Above Water, her autobiography.
Title (italicized) , comma after each title
Rule 4

Wrong Answers

What is this rule?

Some answer choices can be crossed out instantly — before you even read the passage. These four traps appear on almost every SAT Grammar section.

1
Trap #1
Period = Semicolon → Both are wrong
A period and a semicolon do the exact same job: they separate two complete sentences. If you see both in your answer choices, eliminate both — the SAT never makes you choose between two options that work identically.
A.  ...the results. The study...
B.  ...the results; the study...
→ Eliminate A and B immediately. Neither can be correct.
2
Trap #2
No comma before and, or, or but → Wrong
On the SAT, a coordinating conjunction joining two independent clauses always needs a comma before it. If an answer choice has and, or, or but with no comma in front, cross it out.
C.  ...she studied hard but she failed...
D.  ...she studied hard, but she failed...
Quick check: Scan for and / or / but in each choice. No comma right before it? Gone.
3
Trap #3
Transition not hugged by punctuation → Wrong
You learned this in Rule 2: a transition word (however, therefore, though, etc.) must be hugged by punctuation on both sides. If a choice has a naked transition, eliminate it.
A.  ...the plan, however the results...
B.  ...the plan, however, the results... (or ...however; the results...)
4
Trap #4
Identity questions → Always pick the choice without commas
Whenever a question includes a full name (first + last), it's an identity question. The answer will never have commas around the name — commas would imply the name is extra information, but a full name is always essential.
✗  First president, George Washington, was a great man.
✓  Spanish painter Pablo Picasso revolutionized art.
Spot it fast: See a full name in the passage or choices? Find the one without commas around it — that's your answer.
Rule 5

Dangling Participles

1
The Rule
Opening clause → comma → subject
When a sentence begins with a clause that can't stand on its own, the subject of the sentence must come immediately after the comma. The opening clause must logically refer to that subject — otherwise the participle is left "dangling."
[ Opening clause ] , Subject + rest of sentence.
2
Examples
The subject must match the opening clause
In each example below, ask yourself: what does the opening clause refer to? The answer should be the word right after the comma.
Wearing a blue shirt, Juan came in.
→ Who was wearing the blue shirt? Juan was. ✓
Having written many books, Shakespeare is considered a great writer.
→ Who wrote many books? Shakespeare did. ✓
Launched in 1926, the SAT is a standardized test common in the US.
→ What was launched in 1926? The SAT was. ✓
💡
Tip
Always ask: Do I see full sentences as answer choices? If so, what or who is this about?
After reading the opening clause, pause and ask yourself what it's describing. Then check: is that thing the very first word (or words) after the comma? If not, the answer choice is wrong.
On the SAT: Dangling participle questions give you four answer choices that all complete the sentence differently. Find the one where the subject immediately after the comma matches the opening clause. Bonus! It's the only grammar question type that uses full sentences as answer choices, so it's easy to identify.
Rule 6

Suspicions

👁
The Pattern
One period, colon, or semicolon by itself? That's your suspect
The SAT is written so that if exactly one answer choice contains a period, a semicolon, or a colon — and the others don't — that lone choice is very likely the correct one.
One choice has a ; and the rest don't → suspect the semicolon choice
One choice has a : and the rest don't → suspect the colon choice
One choice has a . and the rest don't → suspect the period choice
✓
How to use it
Spot it → read it → confirm it
When you notice one answer with a unique punctuation mark, mentally pick it as your working answer. Then read the full sentence with that choice inserted and make sure it actually makes sense. If it does, you're done.
Important: This is a shortcut, not a guarantee. Always read the sentence through to confirm — the suspicion just tells you where to look first.
The reason this matters so much is that many different question types will appear on the SAT — transitions, supercommas, identity questions, and more. But the suspicious lone period, colon, or semicolon is accurate most of the time.
